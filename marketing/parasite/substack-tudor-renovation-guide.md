# How to Remodel a Twin Cities Tudor Without Erasing What Makes It a Tudor

There is a specific kind of heartbreak that lives in Minneapolis real estate listings. You scroll past a 1928 Linden Hills Tudor with the steep clipped gable, the arched oak door, the original leaded casements, and then you click through to the kitchen photos. White shaker cabinets. Quartz waterfall island. Matte black pulls. A subway tile backsplash that could have been ordered from any catalog in any city in any year since 2015.

The house has been remodeled. The Tudor has been erased.

This is not a screed against shaker cabinets. I install them all the time. The issue is that the Twin Cities has one of the densest, best-preserved concentrations of 1920s and early 1930s English Revival housing in the country, and we are losing the interior character of those houses at a faster rate than we are losing the exteriors. The exteriors are protected by stucco repair costs and the simple fact that nobody wants to mess with a slate roof. The interiors are protected by nothing except the taste of whoever owns the house this decade.

I want to talk about how to remodel one of these houses, specifically the kitchen, without flattening the thing that made you want it in the first place.

## Where the Tudors actually are

If you are looking at a map of Twin Cities Tudor density, four neighborhoods sit at the top.

**Linden Hills.** The blocks between Lake Harriet and Lake Calhoun (Bde Maka Ska) were largely platted and built out between 1915 and 1935. Drive Sheridan, Drew, Chowen, Beard, or Abbott between roughly 40th and 47th and you are looking at an unbroken run of Tudor Revival, Cotswold cottage, and English Country variants. The density here is significant enough that the neighborhood reads as a single architectural moment.

**Kenwood.** Larger lots, larger Tudors. The houses around Kenwood Park and along Mount Curve trend toward the grand end of the style. Half-timbering is more common, the rooflines more complicated, the chimneys more sculptural. These were built for executives of the railroad and milling families, and the budgets show.

**Lowry Hill.** Mixed stock, but the Tudor inventory here is some of the oldest and most architect-designed in the city. You see more individual statements and fewer pattern-book builds.

**Edina Country Club District.** The 1924 Thorpe Bros. development was explicitly marketed as a curated English village. Deed restrictions originally required certain architectural styles, and Tudor dominated. The district is on the National Register, which constrains exterior changes but does almost nothing to protect interiors.

There are pockets of Tudors elsewhere, Tangletown, Fulton, parts of St. Paul's Macalester-Groveland, but those four neighborhoods are where most of the remodeling money is flowing right now.

## What makes a Tudor a Tudor on the inside

Before we talk about what to keep, it helps to name what is actually there. A typical Twin Cities Tudor kitchen, as originally built, was small. Sometimes shockingly small. Servants were assumed in the larger Kenwood houses. In the more modest Linden Hills builds, kitchens were workrooms, not gathering spaces, and they were tucked at the back of the house behind a swinging door.

The character elements that survived into the present, if you are lucky, are these:

- Plaster walls with slightly irregular surfaces and rounded corners at openings
- Coved ceilings or simple plaster cornices
- Quarter-sawn white oak or red oak floors, often with a darker border inlay
- Original cast iron radiators, often with decorative casting
- Arched doorways between rooms, sometimes with a slight Gothic point
- Leaded or diamond-pane casement windows in the breakfast nook
- A butler's pantry or pass-through with original cabinetry and a small sink
- A back staircase to the second floor

Almost none of these elements are easy to replicate. The plaster is the big one. Once you rip it out, you cannot get it back. Modern drywall, even skim-coated and corner-beaded by a careful crew, reads differently. The light hits it differently. You can feel the change even if you cannot name it.

## The five principles

After enough Tudor kitchens, you start to see a pattern in the projects that age well versus the ones that look dated within five years. The ones that age well follow some version of these five rules.

**1. Keep at least one architectural feature from the original kitchen, even if you have to work around it.**

The original swinging door. The butler's pantry. The breakfast nook with leaded windows. The arched opening to the dining room. Pick at least one and design the rest of the kitchen to feature it rather than apologize for it. If you erase everything, you have a generic kitchen in a Tudor shell, and the disconnect will quietly bother you for as long as you live there.

**2. Match cabinet proportions to the house, not to the Pinterest board.**

Tudor kitchens were not designed for forty-two inch wall cabinets and ten foot islands. The rooms are smaller, the ceilings are often lower in the kitchen than in the public rooms, and the visual rhythm of the original house runs at a tighter scale. Oversized cabinets in a small Tudor kitchen read as a costume. Consider inset cabinetry, which was the period-correct construction method, and consider stopping wall cabinets short of the ceiling with a plaster cove or open shelf transition.

**3. Use materials the house would recognize.**

Quarter-sawn white oak. Soapstone. Honed marble. Unlacquered brass that will tarnish. Subway tile is fine if you use the smaller three by six format rather than the larger contemporary sizes, and if you use a real grout color rather than stark white. Avoid anything that screams 2020s: waterfall edges, matte black everything, oversized format porcelain that mimics marble, RGB undercabinet lighting.

**4. Resist the open concept reflex.**

This is the hardest one. Almost every Tudor remodel I get called about starts with the homeowner saying they want to open the kitchen up to the dining room or the living room. Sometimes that is the right move. More often, it destroys the spatial logic of the house. Tudors were designed around the idea of distinct rooms with distinct purposes connected by deliberate openings. The arched doorway between the kitchen and dining room is doing real work, both architecturally and acoustically. Before you remove it, sit in the house for a season and ask whether the problem is really the wall, or whether the problem is that the kitchen is laid out poorly within its existing footprint.

**5. If you do open things up, do it with a Tudor vocabulary.**

If you decide the wall has to come down, frame the new opening as an arch, not a flat header. Use plaster returns, not drywall. Carry the original floor through the opening if you can match it. The goal is to make the change look like it could have been original, or at least like it was done with respect for what was original.

## On removing walls

I am going to be careful here because every house is different and I cannot give you load-bearing advice from a newsletter. The general guidance is this.

Most Tudors in the Twin Cities have a load path that runs from the ridge of the roof down through a center bearing wall, often roughly where the kitchen meets the dining room or the front hall. That wall is frequently the one homeowners want to remove. It can be done, but it requires a structural engineer's stamped drawing, a properly sized LVL or steel beam, and posts that carry the load down to the foundation, which often means opening up the basement to add a footing.

In Minneapolis, this kind of work requires permits through CPED, the city's Community Planning and Economic Development department. You will need a building permit for structural changes, an electrical permit, a plumbing permit if you are moving water or gas, and a mechanical permit if you are touching HVAC. Inspections happen at framing, rough-in, and final. The permit process is straightforward but not fast, and trying to skip it will haunt you when you sell the house and the inspector flags unpermitted work.

Edina has its own permitting process through the city, and the Country Club District has additional review for exterior changes. Interior kitchen remodels generally do not trigger the historic district review, but anything that changes a window opening or the exterior envelope will.

Budget time for permits. A straightforward kitchen permit can move in a couple of weeks. Anything structural or anything in a historic overlay can take longer.

## What this actually costs

Homeowners ask me for a price range before they ask me anything else, so let me give you the honest one.

A cosmetic refresh of a Tudor kitchen, meaning new cabinets in the existing footprint, new counters, new appliances, new lighting, no wall changes, no plumbing relocation, will generally land in the **65,000 to 110,000** range. That assumes mid to upper-mid cabinetry, real stone counters, decent but not luxury appliances, and a crew that knows how to work in plaster without destroying it.

A meaningful remodel, meaning some layout changes, possibly a relocated sink or range, a refreshed butler's pantry, restored or replaced windows in the breakfast nook, upgraded electrical to handle modern appliance loads, will generally land in the **110,000 to 175,000** range.

A full gut with structural work, opening the kitchen to an adjacent room, adding a proper island, period-correct inset cabinetry, high-end appliances, custom range hood, restored or matched plaster, refinished floors throughout the affected area, will generally land in the **175,000 to 250,000** range, sometimes higher in Kenwood or Country Club houses where finish expectations are higher.

These numbers assume you are working in the Twin Cities in the current labor market. They will move with material costs and with the specifics of the house. A house that has had its mechanicals updated in the last fifteen years will cost less to remodel than one with knob and tube wiring and a sixty year old boiler. A house with intact original cabinetry that can be refurbished rather than replaced can cost dramatically less.

The number that surprises people is the cost of doing the work right in a plaster house. Drywall crews charge X per square foot. Plaster crews charge meaningfully more, and there are fewer of them. Matching an original plaster cove around a new opening is real money, but it is the difference between a Tudor and a Tudor-shaped house.

## The honest tradeoffs

There are real reasons people open up Tudor kitchens, and I do not want to pretend those reasons are illegitimate.

The original kitchens were too small for the way most families actually live now. The lighting was minimal. The electrical service was sized for an icebox and a single overhead bulb. The breakfast nook is charming but seats four people uncomfortably. The butler's pantry holds dishes nobody uses.

You are allowed to change your house. You bought it. The argument is not that every original detail is sacred. The argument is that the original details are the reason the house has a personality, and personality is the thing the rest of the city is losing fastest.

The best Tudor remodels I have seen are the ones where the homeowner could articulate, before any design work started, what they loved about the house and what they wanted to keep. The worst ones are the ones where the homeowner started by collecting kitchen photos on Pinterest and then tried to retrofit those photos into a 1927 floor plan.

Start with the house. Let the house tell you what it wants to be. Then bring in the appliances and the islands and the lighting.

## A short note on Linden Hills specifically

If you are remodeling a Tudor in Linden Hills, you have a particular constraint that homeowners in Kenwood or Country Club do not have. Linden Hills lots are narrow. The houses are close together. Side yard setbacks are tight. This means kitchen additions, which are sometimes the cleanest way to get more space without destroying the original interior, are often constrained or impossible without a variance.

The variance process in Minneapolis is doable but slow, and neighbors get notified. If you are planning an addition, talk to the neighbors before you file. A lot of Linden Hills variance requests die in neighborhood opposition that could have been defused with a conversation over the fence.

If an addition is off the table, your options are working within the footprint or annexing space from an adjacent room. Annexing the butler's pantry into the kitchen is the most common move. Annexing the back staircase is the most regrettable one, because that staircase is doing more than you think, both architecturally and for the resale value of the house.

## Closing

The Twin Cities Tudor inventory is one of the quiet treasures of American residential architecture, and most of the protection it has is informal. There is no inspector knocking on your door if you rip out the plaster. There is no fine for replacing the leaded casements with a vinyl slider. The only thing standing between these houses and the slow flattening of their interiors is the taste and patience of the people who own them.

If you own one, you are a steward whether you want to be or not. The remodel you do, or do not do, will outlast you. Do it with some humility about what the house already is, and the next owner will thank you.
