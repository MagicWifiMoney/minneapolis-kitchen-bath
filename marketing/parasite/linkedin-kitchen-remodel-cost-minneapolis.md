# What a Kitchen Remodel Actually Costs in Minneapolis in 2026 (A Contractor's Honest Breakdown)

I have been running kitchen and bath jobs in the Twin Cities for the better part of two decades. In that time I have torn out more 1920s Linden Hills galley kitchens, 1950s Richfield ranch kitchens, and 1990s Maple Grove builder specials than I can count.

And almost every first conversation starts the same way. A homeowner sits across from me at their kitchen table, pulls up a Pinterest board, and asks the same question.

"What is this actually going to cost?"

So let me answer that the way I wish more contractors would. Honestly, with ranges instead of magic numbers, and with the real reasons Minneapolis kitchens cost what they do in 2026.

If you are budgeting a remodel this year, this is the breakdown I would give a friend.

## Why Minneapolis kitchens cost what they do

Before we get to numbers, you have to understand why a kitchen remodel in Minneapolis is structurally more expensive than the same square footage in, say, a Sun Belt new build.

Three reasons.

**One. Our housing stock is old.**

A huge chunk of Minneapolis proper sits in neighborhoods built between 1900 and 1940. Think Powderhorn, Longfellow, Nokomis, Northeast, Kingfield, Linden Hills. The median home in the city is decades older than the national median. That means knob and tube wiring lurking behind plaster, galvanized supply lines, undersized panels, balloon framing, and zero insulation in exterior walls.

You do not see any of that until demo day. And you cannot legally cover it back up once you do.

**Two. Permits and inspections are real here.**

Minneapolis CPED, the city's Community Planning and Economic Development office, takes permits seriously. Saint Paul's Department of Safety and Inspections does too. Suburban cities like Edina, Minnetonka, Bloomington, and Saint Louis Park each have their own building departments with their own quirks. A licensed GC in this market is pricing in the time it takes to pull permits, schedule rough-in inspections, and pass final.

If a quote feels suspiciously cheap, ask whether permits are included. Often they are not.

**Three. Contractor density is lower than people think.**

We have plenty of remodelers in the Twin Cities, but the pool of licensed, insured, properly capitalized GCs who actually show up on time has not kept pace with demand. Post-2020 the backlog stretched into next-year territory for the better shops. It has loosened a bit in 2026, but good crews are still booked three to six months out, and they price accordingly.

Add to that our compressed building season. Yes, we work year-round indoors, but exterior tie-ins, dumpster logistics, and material deliveries all get harder from December through March. Subs charge for that.

Okay. Now the numbers.

## Tier one: the refresh, roughly $15K to $30K

This is the tier most homeowners actually want when they say "I just want to update it."

You are keeping the existing footprint. Same sink location, same range location, same window. You are not moving walls, not relocating plumbing, not touching the panel.

What that budget realistically buys you in Minneapolis in 2026:

- Cabinet refacing or stock cabinets from a big-box or a mid-tier line, installed
- Quartz or entry-level granite counters, fabricated and installed
- A new sink, faucet, garbage disposal
- New backsplash, typically a standard subway or a basic mosaic
- Like-for-like appliance swap, mid-range
- Paint, lighting fixture updates, new hardware
- Permits where required, basic disposal, and labor

What it does not buy you. Custom cabinetry. Moving the island. A new electrical panel. Hardwood floor refinishing throughout. Structural changes. High-end appliances.

This tier works beautifully for a 1990s townhome in Plymouth or a 1970s split-level in Brooklyn Park where the bones are fine and the layout is workable. It is not the right tier for a 1925 bungalow in Standish where the wiring is original and the floor is sagging toward the basement stairs.

If you are in that bungalow situation and someone quotes you $22,000 for a full remodel, ask hard questions about what is excluded.

## Tier two: the mid-range remodel, roughly $35K to $75K

This is where most of my Twin Cities clients actually land.

You are doing a real remodel. You might be taking down a non-load-bearing wall between the kitchen and dining room, which is the single most common request I get in older Minneapolis homes. You are updating electrical to current code in the kitchen footprint. You are likely refinishing or replacing floors. You are getting semi-custom cabinets.

What this tier gets you:

- Semi-custom cabinetry, soft-close everything, a few specialty pull-outs
- Mid-tier quartz, possibly a waterfall edge on an island
- A real designed lighting plan with recessed cans, under-cabinet, and a statement fixture
- New plumbing rough-in if you are moving the sink or adding a pot filler
- Updated electrical, including dedicated circuits for the range, microwave, disposal, and dishwasher per current code
- A mid-range appliance package, often a counter-depth fridge and a gas or induction range
- Tile or stone backsplash with some design intent
- Refinished hardwoods or new LVP throughout the kitchen
- Permits, inspections, dumpster, protection of adjacent spaces, and project management

Timeline on a tier two job runs eight to fourteen weeks from demo to punch list, assuming nothing major is hiding in the walls.

This is the tier that returns the most value when you sell. A well-executed mid-range kitchen in a desirable Minneapolis neighborhood will recoup a healthy share of its cost at resale, especially in zip codes where the median home value sits comfortably in the mid six figures.

## Tier three: the high-end remodel, $80K to $150K and up

This is a footprint change. You are moving walls, possibly load-bearing ones. You are relocating plumbing stacks. You are upgrading the electrical panel. You may be punching out for a larger window or adding a structural beam to open the kitchen to the living room.

What this tier gets you:

- Full custom cabinetry from a local shop, often with inset doors and specialty wood
- Premium stone counters, natural quartzite or high-end quartz, sometimes a hand-selected slab
- Professional-grade appliances, the Sub-Zero and Wolf and Miele tier
- Custom range hood, often a plaster or metal-clad statement piece
- Structural work, beam installation, possible foundation considerations
- Full electrical updates, often including a panel upgrade
- HVAC modifications if you are opening up the floor plan
- High-end tile, designer lighting, integrated smart home wiring
- A real designer on the project from day one
- Permits, structural engineering stamps, multiple inspection cycles

The $80K floor on this tier assumes you are not doing an addition. The moment you push out the exterior envelope, you are in a different conversation, often well north of $200K once you account for footings, framing, roofing tie-in, siding match, and city setback variance work.

This tier makes sense in homes where the existing kitchen is genuinely undersized for the property value. Think a $1.2M home in Linden Hills with a cramped 1920s galley. The market expects more, and the investment supports it.

## The hidden cost lines nobody mentions

Here is where homeowners get burned. These are line items that rarely show up in the first quote but almost always show up on the final invoice.

**Knob and tube remediation.** If your home was built before 1945 and has not been fully rewired, expect to find some. Replacing it in the kitchen footprint alone can add $2,000 to $6,000. Beyond the kitchen, more.

**Panel upgrades.** Many older Minneapolis homes still run 100-amp service. A modern kitchen with induction, an electric oven, and a heat-pump water heater needs 200 amps. Budget $2,500 to $5,000 for the upgrade, plus utility coordination with Xcel.

**Asbestos and lead.** Pre-1978 homes can have lead paint. Pre-1980 floor tile and mastic can contain asbestos. Testing is cheap. Abatement is not. Budget a contingency.

**Floor leveling.** Older homes settle. If you are installing large-format tile or a long run of LVP, the subfloor often needs leveling compound or sistered joists. Add $1,000 to $4,000.

**Soffit and bulkhead surprises.** That soffit above the upper cabinets often hides a duct, a vent stack, or a beam you cannot remove without an engineer.

**HVAC rebalancing.** Open up a wall between two rooms and your existing forced-air system may not heat or cool the new space evenly. A zoning fix or a duct rework runs $1,500 to $5,000.

**Permit timing.** Minneapolis and Saint Paul both have busy permit queues. A two-week delay on a permit is a two-week delay on your job, and your contractor's crew may pivot to another project in the meantime. Build a buffer.

I tell every client to hold a contingency of 10 to 15 percent of the contract value, on top of the contract value, for exactly these surprises. On older homes I push it to 20 percent.

## A simple decision tree

If you are trying to figure out which tier is right for you, here is the framework I use with new clients.

**Start with the home's current value and your time horizon.**

If you plan to sell within three years and the home is valued below the neighborhood median, stay in tier one or the low end of tier two. Do not over-improve. The market will not pay you back.

If you plan to stay seven or more years, and the home is at or above the neighborhood median, tier two is almost always the right answer. You get a kitchen you love, and you do not overcapitalize.

If the home is in the top quartile of its zip code, the kitchen is genuinely holding the property back, and you are staying long-term, tier three is justifiable. Just go in with eyes open about what 80 to 150 grand actually buys.

**Then check the bones.**

If your home is pre-1940 and has not had recent electrical or plumbing updates, add a tier in your head before you start. The hidden work is real, and pretending it is not will blow up your budget mid-project.

**Then pressure-test the timeline.**

If you cannot live without a kitchen for eight to fourteen weeks, you are not ready for tier two or three. Plan for a refresh, or plan to rent a short-term place during the build.
